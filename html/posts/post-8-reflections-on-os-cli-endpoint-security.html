Day 6 at ReDI: Operating Systems, Command Line & Endpoint Security

By Oriana • Apr 29 2026 • 4 min read • #security #operating-systems #ReDI #endpoint #capstone-project

As I continue my journey in cybersecurity, I’ve been reflecting on the fundamentals that shape how we interact with technology every day—operating systems, the way we interface with machines, and the importance of securing the devices we rely on .

Operating Systems: The Quiet Backbone of Everything

Operating systems are the layer that makes computers usable, managing hardware, software, and everything in between. Exploring the different categories—desktop, server, mobile, networking, and embedded OS—has shown me how each one supports a different part of our digital world.

Desktop systems like Windows and macOS are designed for usability but often become prime targets for malware. Server OS need strong security because they run critical services. Mobile and embedded systems, especially in IoT devices, are everywhere now, yet they’re often overlooked when it comes to updates and security hardening.

Learning about OS types—batch, multiprogramming, distributed, embedded, and real-time—also gave me a better sense of how diverse environments really are. Legacy batch systems still run in industries like finance, while real-time OS are essential in places where timing and reliability can’t fail, like hospitals.

A Real-World Example: Hospital IT Audit

A hospital is a perfect example of how many different operating systems can coexist in one environment. Windows desktops for staff, real-time OS in patient monitors, distributed systems handling cloud storage—each one has its own risks and its own security needs. What stands out most is that in healthcare, protecting patient safety and sensitive data always comes first, especially when some systems can’t be patched or monitored easily.

CLI vs GUI: Two Different Mindsets

I’ve always preferred the command line, and the more I work in cybersecurity, the more that preference makes sense. GUIs are useful for quick visual tasks, but the CLI gives me the speed, precision, and direct control that technical work demands. It’s the environment where real troubleshooting happens and where both attackers and defenders operate, so being comfortable in it isn’t just a preference — it’s a practical advantage.

Endpoint Security: The First and Last Line of Defense

Endpoint security focuses on protecting the devices we use every day from threats like malware, ransomware, and unauthorized access. Tools like antivirus, EDR, firewalls, and disk encryption are important, but the real strength comes from understanding what’s happening under the surface. Checking processes, open ports, logs, and permissions gives you a clearer picture of system activity. Since attackers use the same tools and techniques, defenders need to be equally skilled and proactive.
